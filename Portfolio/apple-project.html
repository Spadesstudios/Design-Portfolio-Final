Context

The Problem Space

Falls are the leading cause of injury-related death among adults over 65, and the risk is dramatically elevated for Alzheimer's and dementia patients — who experience spatial disorientation, impaired balance, and difficulty navigating even familiar environments. Assisted living facilities struggle to monitor and mitigate this risk without compromising resident dignity and independence.

This engagement centered on designing a solution that could be deployed in a resident's personal living space — a HomePod-class device that used ambient technology to actively support safer movement, without the intrusive feel of surveillance or institutional monitoring equipment.

"The user isn't choosing this technology. It's chosen for them by people who care about them. Designing for that reality changes everything."

My Role

Responsibilities on the Engagement

I was brought in to drive the product and UX work — bridging the gap between user needs research and the technical product definition. My scope of work spanned the full design process from user research through validated prototypes.

  • Led user research sessions with Alzheimer's patients, caregivers, and assisted living facility staff
  • Synthesized research into behavioral insights and design principles specific to this user population
  • Developed user journey maps covering both the resident and caregiver experience
  • Defined product requirements and feature prioritization in collaboration with engineering
  • Designed interaction models for ambient, non-screen-dependent experiences
  • Created and tested prototypes with actual users in assisted living environments
  • Delivered product specifications, interaction guidelines, and research documentation
Design Process

How We Approached It

This project demanded a research-first approach. Designing for Alzheimer's patients means designing for users who cannot always advocate for themselves, may not remember their experience during testing, and respond to the world very differently than a typical technology user. Every assumption required validation.

Design Challenges

What Made This Hard

This engagement surfaced design challenges I hadn't encountered in previous product work — each requiring a fundamentally different design lens than typical consumer product work.

User Advocacy

Alzheimer's patients often cannot articulate their needs or evaluate their experience. Research required observation over self-reporting, and design validation required proxy feedback from caregivers combined with direct behavioral observation.

Dignity by Design

Safety technology in elder care often erodes dignity — residents feel monitored, infantilized, or surveilled. Every design decision had to balance safety function with preservation of personal autonomy and dignity.

Multi-User Experience

The product served three distinct users: the resident, the caregiver, and the facility administrator — each with fundamentally different needs, mental models, and comfort levels with technology.

Ambient Interaction

Designing a device that helps without requiring active engagement — the user shouldn't have to think about the technology for it to work. Ambient interaction design is a distinct discipline with little room for error.

Outcomes

What Was Achieved

Due to NDA obligations, specific quantitative outcomes, product details, and client-specific results cannot be disclosed. At a high level, the engagement concluded with:

  • A validated product concept with strong research backing across multiple user groups
  • Comprehensive UX documentation including journey maps, interaction specs, and design principles
  • A tested prototype that demonstrated measurable improvement in target safety outcomes during validation sessions
  • Research findings that meaningfully shaped the product roadmap for subsequent development phases
  • Cross-functional alignment between design, engineering, and clinical advisory stakeholders

"This was the most human-centered design work I've ever done. When the stakes are someone's safety and dignity, design rigor isn't optional."

Reflection

What This Taught Me

Working on this project fundamentally changed how I think about user research and design ethics. It forced me to develop a deeper empathy practice — one grounded in observation and behavior rather than stated preferences — and to hold both the functional and emotional dimensions of a product with equal weight. It's the kind of product work I want to do more of.
